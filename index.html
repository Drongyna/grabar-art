Father & Son

N° I

The Father · Classical Master

Grabar Y.

Born 1946, seventy-nine years in paint

Grabar Y. came of age during a time when painting was still an act of devotion — slow, meditative, deeply attentive to the world. Trained in the classical tradition, he spent decades studying light as it falls across a human face, or lingers on the edge of a hillside at dusk.

I do not paint what I see. I paint what remains when I close my eyes.

His portraits are distinguished by their psychological depth — each subject rendered not merely in likeness but in essence. His landscapes carry a sense of earned stillness, as if the earth itself has consented to be observed. Working primarily in oils, he favors the muted palette of the old masters: umbers, ochres, the cold white of winter sky.

Over five decades, he has exhibited across Ukraine and Eastern Europe, his work held in private collections from Lviv to Vienna. Now in his seventy-ninth year, he continues to paint every morning.

Oil on Canvas Portraiture Landscape Old Masters
N° II

The Son · Abstractionist

Grabar R.

Born 1969, fifty-six years of colour and form

Growing up surrounded by his father's quiet canvases, Grabar R. absorbed the discipline of observation — then exploded it. Where his father found poetry in restraint, the son discovered it in rupture: vivid acid pigments, restless compositions, colour relationships that feel almost adversarial.

My father taught me to see. I paint what happens when seeing becomes feeling.

Grabar R. works in a vocabulary that is unmistakably of his generation: large-format canvases layered with acrylics, oils, and mixed media. His abstract works operate on a physical register — they are meant to be felt as much as seen, their vibrating colour fields demanding something from the viewer in return.

He has shown extensively across Ukraine, Poland, and Germany. His bold, chromatic style has made him a distinctive voice in contemporary Ukrainian art.

Acrylic Abstract Mixed Media Large Format
❦

A Family Legacy

Two Hands, One Story

What makes the Grabar story singular is not merely the coincidence of talent across generations, but the dialogue between two radically different visions. Father and son rarely agree on what painting should do — and yet their works, hung side by side, reveal something neither could achieve alone.
